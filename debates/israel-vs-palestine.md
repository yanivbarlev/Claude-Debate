## Q1: Who has the stronger historical and legal claim to the land?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
Israel holds the stronger historical and legal claim based on a convergence of ancient history, modern legal instruments, and international recognition. The Jewish people maintained an unbroken presence in the land for over 3,000 years, with Jerusalem serving as the capital of the Davidic kingdom around 1000 BCE. Even during centuries of diaspora, Jewish communities remained in Hebron, Safed, Tiberias, and Jerusalem continuously.

On the legal front, the 1917 Balfour Declaration committed Britain to a Jewish homeland. The 1920 San Remo Conference, a binding act of international law, allocated the Mandate for Palestine specifically to facilitate that homeland. The League of Nations Mandate of 1922 incorporated these obligations, making Zionist settlement legally sanctioned under international authority. The UN Partition Plan of 1947 (Resolution 181) further affirmed the legitimacy of a Jewish state.

Israel's Declaration of Independence in May 1948 was immediately recognized by the United States and Soviet Union. By 1949, Israel was admitted to the United Nations as a full member state. Over 160 nations today recognize Israel. No equivalent international legal architecture exists for any rival claim to the entire territory. The legal and historical record supports Israel's foundational right to exist and govern within recognized borders.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
Palestinians hold the stronger claim under both historical continuity and modern international law. Arab Palestinians constituted roughly 90% of the population in Ottoman Palestine as late as 1878, with Jewish residents comprising approximately 8%. This demographic reality reflects centuries of continuous habitation, not a claim based on ancient scripture or diaspora longing.

The 1947 UN Partition Plan (Resolution 181) proposed dividing land where Palestinians were the overwhelming majority — yet even that plan allocated 56% of the territory to a Jewish state. Israel's declaration of statehood in 1948 preceded any negotiated agreement, and the subsequent Nakba displaced over 700,000 Palestinians from their homes and villages.

International law is unambiguous on the post-1967 situation: UN Security Council Resolution 242 (1967) and Resolution 338 (1973) call for Israeli withdrawal from occupied territories, affirming that acquisition of land by force is inadmissible. The International Court of Justice's 2004 advisory opinion confirmed that Israel's occupation violates foundational norms of international humanitarian law.

The Balfour Declaration of 1917 was a colonial-era promise made by a British government that had no sovereign right to gift Palestinian land. Biblical or ancient historical claims cannot override modern property rights, population demographics, and the post-1945 international legal order — which was designed precisely to prevent conquest-based dispossession.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
The demographic argument is a red herring. Ottoman census data was notoriously unreliable, and "Palestine" had no distinct political identity — it was a collection of administrative districts. More critically, the 1947 Partition Plan was accepted by Jewish leadership and rejected outright by Arab states, who then launched a war to destroy the proposed Jewish state. The Nakba was a consequence of that Arab-initiated war, not a premeditated ethnic cleansing program.

UNSC 242 calls for negotiated withdrawal, not unconditional withdrawal — the deliberate omission of "all" territories by its drafters, including Lord Caradon, was intentional. Biblical claims are not Israel's primary legal foundation — the 1922 Mandate, 1947 UN partition, and 1948 declaration under international recognition are the pillars. The legal and historical record is not ambiguous.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
Continuous Jewish presence does not equal exclusive sovereign claim — these are entirely distinct legal concepts. The Balfour Declaration was a colonial instrument that never consulted the 90% Arab majority. UN Resolution 181's legitimacy is undermined by the fact that the General Assembly lacks authority to partition territory against its inhabitants' will — this is settled UN Charter law.

The 700,000 Palestinians expelled in 1948 also have ancestral connection to that land. Archaeological presence does not extinguish the rights of people living there in 1947. 160 nations recognizing Israel is irrelevant to whether Palestinian rights were violated — 160 nations also recognize the right of Palestinians to self-determination per repeated UNGA resolutions.

## Q2: Is the military occupation and Gaza blockade justified on security grounds?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
The answer is unequivocally yes — context and consequences demand it. Israel withdrew every soldier and settler from Gaza in 2005, a unilateral peace gesture. Hamas seized control in 2007, immediately using the territory as a launch pad for thousands of rockets against Israeli civilians. The blockade was a direct and proportionate response to an armed group committed by its own charter to Israel's destruction.

The Second Intifada (2000-2005) killed over 1,000 Israeli civilians in suicide bombings on buses, in cafes, and at Passover seders. The security barrier, completed mostly by 2004, reduced suicide attacks by over 90 percent. That is not occupation for its own sake — that is a democracy protecting its citizens from mass murder.

Under international humanitarian law, a state has not only the right but the obligation to defend its population. The October 7, 2023 attack killed 1,200 people and took 250 hostages. That attack was planned and launched from territory Israel had vacated. Security measures in the West Bank exist precisely to prevent a repeat.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
The security justification collapses under empirical scrutiny. Israel has occupied the West Bank since 1967 — nearly six decades — and imposed its Gaza blockade since 2007. If security were truly the objective, 59 years of occupation with no resolution indicates the strategy has failed by its own metric.

The Gaza blockade restricts not just weapons but food, medicine, construction materials, and civilian movement. UN documents from 2012 warned Gaza would be "unlivable" by 2020 — a prediction that proved accurate. Internal Israeli documents from 2010 (revealed via FOI) showed officials calculated minimum calories to avoid malnutrition — collective punishment prohibited under Article 33 of the Fourth Geneva Convention.

The West Bank occupation generates the very radicalization it purports to prevent. Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak acknowledged in 2000 that were he Palestinian, he too would have joined a militant organization. Security rationales that require perpetual dispossession and collective punishment are not security policies — they are occupation management strategies.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
The "calorie calculation" claim is misleading — it refers to a 2008 document estimating subsistence thresholds during a temporary tightening, not a policy of deliberate starvation. The West Bank barrier reduced suicide bombings by over 90% after 2002's 130 Israeli civilian deaths in a single year. Gaza has zero Israeli settlers post-2005 yet produced October 7 — this directly challenges the premise that occupation is the cause of violence. Barak's hypothetical statement is anecdotal speculation, not policy.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
Gaza was never free. The UN, ICRC, and virtually every international legal body classified Gaza as occupied territory even post-2005 because Israel controlled its airspace, coastline, borders, population registry, and electromagnetic sphere. Hamas's rise was partly enabled by Israel's deliberate policy of weakening the PLO, as former security officials acknowledged.

Israel's response to October 7 killed over 50,000 Palestinians in Gaza — the majority civilians and children per UN and WHO data — destroyed 90% of housing, and induced famine. The International Court of Justice ordered Israel to prevent genocide. Self-defense cannot legally justify collective punishment of 2.3 million people; the Fourth Geneva Convention is unambiguous on this.

## Q3: Does Israel meet the criteria of an apartheid state?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
Israel does not meet the legal definition of apartheid. The Rome Statute defines apartheid as an institutionalized regime of systematic oppression by one racial group over another with the intent to maintain domination. Israel's governance structure fails this test on multiple grounds.

Within Israel's 1948 borders, Arab citizens — approximately 21 percent of the population — hold full voting rights, sit in the Knesset, serve as Supreme Court justices, and participate in all professions including the military and diplomacy. In 2021, the Ra'am Party became the first Arab party to join an Israeli governing coalition. No apartheid state has ever integrated its "oppressed" group into the highest levels of government.

The West Bank situation reflects a temporary, conflict-driven security arrangement, not a racial ideology. South Africa's apartheid was a permanent, codified racial system applied to citizens within sovereign borders. Israel applies civilian law to its citizens and military law to a non-citizen population in a contested zone — a legally and morally distinct situation. The apartheid charge is strategically deployed to delegitimize Israel entirely, not to reform specific policies.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
By the legal definition established in the 1973 International Convention on the Suppression and Punishment of the Crime of Apartheid, and elaborated in the Rome Statute, Israel's system qualifies. Apartheid requires institutionalized racial or ethnic domination enforced through systematic oppression — precisely what exists between the Jordan River and Mediterranean Sea.

In the West Bank, Jewish settlers and Palestinian residents live under entirely separate legal systems on the same land. Settlers are governed by Israeli civil law; Palestinians by military law with military courts boasting a 99.7% conviction rate. This legal bifurcation on the basis of ethnicity is apartheid's definitional core.

Major human rights bodies have reached this conclusion: Human Rights Watch (2021), Amnesty International (2022), B'Tselem (2021), and the UN Special Rapporteur. Desmond Tutu visited the West Bank and stated the situation "reminded me of the apartheid regime." Movement restrictions, separate roads, ID card systems, discriminatory land allocation, and the Nation-State Law explicitly privileging Jewish identity are architecture of domination.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
The apartheid label is polemical, not legal. South African apartheid was racial segregation within a single sovereign state. Palestinian Arabs in the West Bank and Gaza are not Israeli citizens — they are stateless populations in disputed territories where final-status negotiations were never completed. Arab citizens of Israel vote, serve in the Knesset, sit on the Supreme Court, and hold military commissions. Military courts exist because the civilian court system has no jurisdiction over non-citizen combatants. Even the International Court of Justice's 2024 advisory opinion did not declare Israel an apartheid state.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
The "Arab citizens vote" defense was used to justify South African Bantustans — formal rights within a discriminatory system do not negate apartheid. Israel's own Nation-State Law (2018) explicitly downgrades Arabic and reserves national self-determination exclusively for Jewish people — codified ethnic hierarchy in Basic Law. The ICJ's 2024 advisory opinion declared the occupation itself illegal and called for its termination within 12 months — a historic condemnation. When millions in the West Bank live under military law while settlers on the same land live under civil law, that is legal segregation by ethnicity that meets the Rome Statute's intent requirement.

## Q4: Are Palestinian armed resistance movements justified under international law?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
This question demands precision. International law permits armed resistance by peoples under occupation against military targets — it does not authorize deliberate mass murder of civilians. Hamas's October 7 attack, which targeted a music festival, kibbutz homes, and families — executing children, raping women, and burning people alive — has no legal or moral justification under any framework of international humanitarian law.

Common Article 3 of the Geneva Conventions explicitly prohibits violence against civilians, hostage-taking, and outrages upon personal dignity. Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad have systematically violated every one of them. Firing unguided rockets into civilian population centers — over 20,000 since 2001 — is a war crime regardless of claimed political purpose.

The "liberation struggle" framing also ignores that Israel repeatedly offered statehood. In 2000 at Camp David and 2008, Olmert offered a Palestinian state. When a negotiated state is repeatedly offered and refused, the "liberation" framing loses credibility. Armed groups that reject diplomacy, target civilians, and use their own population as human shields are not liberation movements — they are militant organizations prolonging suffering.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
International law explicitly recognizes the right of peoples under colonial domination and foreign occupation to resist — including by armed means. UN General Assembly Resolution 3314 (1974) and Resolution 37/43 (1982) reaffirm "the legitimacy of the struggle of peoples for independence, territorial integrity, national unity and liberation from colonial and foreign domination and foreign occupation by all available means, including armed struggle."

The same legal and moral framework that justified the French Resistance against Nazi occupation and South African armed resistance against apartheid applies to Palestinians under a military occupation lasting nearly six decades.

The distinction critical to this debate: targeting military occupying forces — protected. Targeting civilians — a war crime. Palestinian factions that target Israeli civilians commit violations that must be condemned. But condemning specific tactics is categorically different from condemning the right to resistance itself. When non-violent resistance is met with live ammunition (2018 Great March of Return: Israeli snipers killed over 200 unarmed protesters), the argument that Palestinians must restrict themselves to non-violence becomes a demand they accept their own subjugation.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
UNGA Resolution 37/43 is a non-binding General Assembly resolution — it carries no force of international law. The French Resistance and ANC did not systematically murder children at music festivals, take civilian hostages including infants, or rape women as deliberate war strategy — October 7 did. Conflating those movements with Hamas is historically illiterate. The 2018 Gaza protests were not uniformly unarmed — IDF documented armed infiltrators and IED placements within protest cover. The right to resist does not extend to deliberate targeting of non-combatants under any credible reading of international humanitarian law, including Common Article 3.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
October 7 atrocities were war crimes — this debate does not require defending them. But the opponent uses Hamas's crimes to retroactively delegitimize Palestinian civilian rights, which is a logical fallacy. Common Article 3 applies equally to Israel's conduct in Gaza — indiscriminate bombing of refugee camps, hospitals, and bakeries also constitutes war crimes under the same Geneva Conventions. Camp David 2000 offered Palestinian cantons disconnected by Israeli roads with Israel retaining sovereignty over borders, airspace, and electromagnetic spectrum — not a viable state. Dennis Ross's own maps confirm this. No party is obligated to accept an offer that falls short of their legal rights under international law.

## Q5: Has the international community been fair and balanced?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
The international community has been profoundly, demonstrably unfair to Israel. The United Nations Human Rights Council has passed more resolutions condemning Israel than all other countries combined — 78 resolutions between 2006 and 2022, compared to 7 for Syria, 6 for North Korea, and 4 for Iran. The UN General Assembly's Agenda Item 7 singles out Israel as the only country with a permanent standing agenda item for condemnation.

The 1975 UN resolution equating Zionism with racism (later revoked in 1991) reveals how political manipulation has historically driven international positions on Israel. Robert Bernstein, founder of Human Rights Watch, resigned in protest over his own organization's disproportionate focus on Israel. The double standard is documented, not a talking point.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
The international community's approach has been profoundly unbalanced, with US power systematically shielding Israel from accountability. The United States has vetoed over 45 UN Security Council resolutions critical of Israel since 1972. In February 2024, the US vetoed a ceasefire resolution while Gaza's death toll surpassed 30,000.

The Oslo Accords required Palestinians to recognize Israel's right to exist, but Israel was never required to recognize Palestinian statehood or halt settlement expansion — which accelerated after Oslo. The EU declares settlements "illegal" but imposes no penalties. The ICC's 2021 investigation was met with US threats of sanctions. Israel receives $3.8 billion annually in US military aid with no human rights conditions. This is the opposite of balance.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
US vetoes reflect geopolitical alliance, not evidence of Palestinian righteousness. The UNHRC's institutional bias is documented — it included members like Libya and Venezuela while uniquely targeting Israel. The Oslo asymmetry reflected reality: Israel was recognizing a non-state actor that had previously committed mass terrorism — a massive concession. Military aid comes with end-use restrictions under the Arms Export Control Act. Imperfect accountability is not proof of impunity.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
The disproportionate UNHRC focus on Israel reflects 57 years of occupation generating visible, reviewable, daily violations. Syria and North Korea obstruct UN investigators entirely — the comparison is invalid. Robert Bernstein's 2009 op-ed argued accountability should apply less to open societies — that is a perverse inversion of democratic accountability, not a principled critique. Criticizing Israeli state policy is not antisemitism — conflating the two suppresses legitimate human rights discourse.

## Q6: Is the two-state solution viable, and who bears responsibility for its failure?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
The two-state solution remains the only viable framework, and the primary responsibility for its failure lies with Palestinian leadership's repeated rejection of credible offers and Hamas's eliminationist ideology.

In 1937, the Peel Commission proposed partition — Jewish leadership accepted, Arab leadership rejected. In 1947, the UN Partition Plan — accepted by Jews, rejected by Arabs who launched war. In 2000, Clinton's parameters at Camp David offered a Palestinian state in Gaza, nearly all of the West Bank, and a shared Jerusalem — rejected. In 2008, Olmert's offer included 93.5% of the West Bank plus land swaps, a Palestinian capital in East Jerusalem — Abbas did not respond.

Hamas's founding charter calls explicitly for the destruction of Israel and the killing of Jews. A two-state solution requires both parties to accept the other's existence. Israeli settlement expansion complicates the path and deserves criticism, but it has not made a state impossible — negotiated land swaps have been proposed repeatedly.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
The two-state solution has been rendered nearly non-viable by deliberate Israeli policy. Over 700,000 Israeli settlers now live in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, strategically placed to fragment Palestinian territory into non-contiguous bantustans, severing East Jerusalem from any future Palestinian capital. The settlement enterprise has accelerated under every Israeli government regardless of party.

Israeli negotiators at Camp David in 2000 did not offer a viable Palestinian state. As Robert Malley, who attended as a US aide, documented: the offer required Palestinian acceptance of Israeli sovereignty over large settlement blocs, no Palestinian control of Jerusalem's holy sites, and no resolution of the refugee question. Hamas emerged in the late 1980s as a direct consequence of occupation, not as its cause. The Palestinian Authority has recognized Israel's right to exist since 1993 — a recognition Israel has never reciprocated regarding Palestinian statehood.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
Gaza has zero Israeli settlers yet produced October 7 — this challenges the premise that settlements are the sole obstacle. Camp David included 91-94% of the West Bank, a Palestinian capital in East Jerusalem, and control of the Temple Mount's surface. Robert Malley himself later acknowledged Palestinian responsibility for Camp David's failure. Hamas emerged from the Muslim Brotherhood's ideological project — radicalization is a choice, not an inevitability of occupation.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
Clayton Swisher's "The Truth About Camp David" and Robert Malley's own Foreign Affairs article documented that no formal final written offer was ever tabled at Camp David. Clinton said Arafat made mistakes but never asserted a complete offer was rejected. The 2017 Hamas charter revision — accepting a Palestinian state on 1967 borders — was entirely ignored by the opponent. The Arab Peace Initiative of 2002 offered 22 Arab states' full normalization for 1967 borders — Israel rejected it without a counter-offer. Actions speak: settlement construction never stopped during any negotiation.

## Q7: How should the Palestinian refugee right of return be addressed?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
The right of return must be addressed through compensation, resettlement in a future Palestinian state, and negotiated family reunification — not through a blanket return to Israel proper that would demographically dismantle the world's only Jewish state.

UNRWA's definition is unique — it passes refugee status down through generations indefinitely, inflating the number from roughly 700,000 original 1948 refugees to over 5 million today. No other refugee population in the world is defined this way.

Approximately 800,000 to 1 million Jewish refugees were expelled from Arab countries after 1948, losing property, businesses, and communities. These refugees were absorbed into Israel and third countries with no UN agency and no calls for return. A just resolution must be symmetrical. Palestinian refugees deserve humanitarian resolution, but the specific right of return to Israel is a political demand designed to eliminate Israel through demographics, not a humanitarian imperative.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
The Palestinian right of return is not a political demand — it is an individual legal right. UN General Assembly Resolution 194 (1948) affirmed that "refugees wishing to return to their homes and live at peace with their neighbours should be permitted to do so at the earliest practicable date." This resolution has been reaffirmed over 130 times.

Approximately 5.9 million Palestinian refugees are registered with UNRWA, descendants of the 750,000+ expelled during the 1948 Nakba. Their villages — over 400 of which were destroyed — are documented by Israeli historian Benny Morris in "The Birth of the Palestinian Refugee Problem." This was not voluntary flight; it was organized expulsion, including documented massacres such as Deir Yassin.

The argument that Palestinian refugee rights cannot be honored because they would alter Israel's Jewish demographic majority explicitly privileges one group's ethnic dominance over another group's legal rights. A just resolution must acknowledge the right and provide practical mechanisms — return, compensation, or resettlement with genuine alternatives and individual choice.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
Resolution 194 has conditionality — "wishing to return and live in peace with their neighbours." Benny Morris — cited by the opponent — concluded in his own work that no master plan for expulsion existed and that some measures were historically understandable in the context of a war of annihilation launched against Israel. The 800,000 Jewish refugees also expelled from Arab countries receive zero international advocacy. UNRWA's multigenerational definition inflates numbers from 700,000 to 5.9 million — a figure that would demographically eliminate the Jewish state by design.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
UNRWA's multigenerational definition exists because the refugees were never resettled — you cannot extinguish a refugee crisis by redefining the victims. The 1951 Refugee Convention itself recognizes inherited refugee status when the original displacement remains unresolved. Regarding Jewish refugees from Arab countries: Israel used those refugees as citizens and built a state around them, while simultaneously arguing Palestinian refugees have no right of return to the state built on their homes. Resolution 194, reaffirmed over 130 times, establishes the right of return as individual and inalienable — not subject to demographic calculations by the dispossessing party.

## Q8: Are Israeli settlements a violation of international law?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
The legal picture is more contested than critics acknowledge. The West Bank's legal status derives from the 1949 armistice lines — not recognized international borders. Jordan, which controlled the West Bank from 1948 to 1967, had no legitimate sovereign claim — it had seized the territory in an offensive war against Israel. When Israel captured the West Bank in 1967 in a defensive war, it entered territory with no recognized sovereign.

Article 49 of the Fourth Geneva Convention, which critics cite, was written specifically in response to Nazi Germany's deportation of populations into occupied territory. Voluntary movement of civilians into disputed territory is legally distinguishable. Former State Department Legal Advisor Stephen Schwebel and Judge Eugene Rostow argued that Israel has valid claims in the West Bank pending a final peace agreement. Resolution 242 did not require full withdrawal from all territories — the deliberate omission of "the" before "territories" was a negotiated ambiguity. Final borders must be determined through negotiation, not unilateral condemnation.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
Israeli settlements in the West Bank are unambiguously illegal under international law. Article 49 of the Fourth Geneva Convention is explicit: "The Occupying Power shall not deport or transfer parts of its own civilian population into the territory it occupies." The International Committee of the Red Cross, the authoritative interpreter of Geneva Conventions, has repeatedly confirmed this prohibition applies to Israeli settlements.

The International Court of Justice in its 2004 advisory opinion stated settlements "have been established in breach of international law." UN Security Council Resolution 2334 (2016) declared settlements have "no legal validity" and constitute "a flagrant violation under international law." Over 700,000 settlers now occupy over 42% of West Bank land. Water resources are diverted from Palestinian communities to settlements at ratios of 6:1. Settlement infrastructure physically fragments Palestinian territory, making a contiguous Palestinian state impossible.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
The ICJ's 2004 advisory opinion was non-binding. UNSC 2334 passed because the Obama administration deliberately abstained — a parting political shot, not a legal transformation of the territories' status. The West Bank's legal status remains "disputed," not "occupied sovereign Palestinian territory," because no Palestinian state has ever exercised sovereignty there. Jordan's 1948-1967 control was itself unrecognized by most states. The Israeli Supreme Court has ordered route modifications and removed illegal outposts, demonstrating functioning judicial oversight.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
Article 49(6) applies regardless of the prior sovereignty question — confirmed explicitly by the ICJ in both 2004 and 2024. The Schwebel-Rostow minority view was rejected by the UN Security Council in Resolutions 446, 452, and 465 — all passed with US support. The French text of Resolution 242 uses "des territoires" implying all territories. Over 700,000 settlers now live in the West Bank while Israel claims the matter is "under negotiation" — this is running out the clock, not preparation for a negotiated solution.

## Q9: What role does governance play in the conflict?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
Israel is a functioning democracy with independent courts, a free press, an active civil society, and civilian oversight of the military. Its Supreme Court regularly rules against the government, including on settlement issues. Arab citizens vote, protest, litigate, and govern. Israel has made peace with Egypt in 1979 and Jordan in 1994, proving its government can deliver durable agreements.

Palestinian governance presents a far more troubling picture. The Palestinian Authority has governed under Mahmoud Abbas since 2005 — without elections since 2006. The PA's "pay-to-slay" policy — Law No. 14 of 2004 — provides monthly salaries to Palestinians convicted of terrorism and their families, spending hundreds of millions of dollars annually on institutionalized incitement.

In Gaza, Hamas has suppressed political opposition, executed rivals, diverted international aid to build tunnels and rockets, and embedded military infrastructure in hospitals, schools, and mosques. No serious pathway to peace is possible when one governing entity rewards murder and another refuses democratic accountability.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
Palestinian governance is deeply flawed — Hamas's 2023 attacks were war crimes, the Palestinian Authority is corrupt and undemocratic. But Palestinian governance failures must be contextualized against structural conditions. The PA was created by Oslo as a subcontractor of Israeli occupation — denied control of borders, airspace, water, and electromagnetic spectrum.

Hamas emerged from the ruins of a peace process Palestinians felt had failed them. When Hamas won the 2006 Palestinian legislative elections — elections the Carter Center declared free and fair — Israel, the US, and EU immediately imposed an economic siege, signaling that democratic outcomes are only accepted when they produce compliant leadership.

Israel's governance, by contrast, operates from a position of overwhelming power. Benjamin Netanyahu has openly stated he blocked the two-state solution as a political objective. The government funds Jewish-only infrastructure on occupied land. Palestinian governance failures occur under occupation; Israeli governance failures are the occupation.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
Yasser Arafat diverted hundreds of millions in international aid into personal accounts and factional patronage — documented by the IMF and World Bank. Hamas won the 2006 election and then violently seized Gaza in 2007, throwing Fatah members off rooftops — the Carter Center certified the vote, not the subsequent coup. The Israeli Supreme Court has ruled against the government on settlements and barrier route issues more times than acknowledged. Blaming occupation for every internal Palestinian governance failure denies Palestinian agency.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
The "pay-to-slay" characterization misrepresents a social welfare system for imprisoned family members — analogous to how many states support families of imprisoned individuals. Palestinian elections were not held partly because Israel arrested elected Hamas legislators and the US refused to recognize results — the democratic process was sabotaged externally.

Egypt 1979 and Jordan 1994 peace deals are irrelevant comparisons: those states made peace over their own sovereign territories. Israel has never offered Palestinians what it offered Egypt — full sovereignty, recognized borders, and end of occupation. The comparison proves the point: when Israel is asked to give the same terms to Palestinians, it refuses.

## Q10: What would a just and lasting peace look like?

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Stance
A just and lasting peace requires two states for two peoples, mutual recognition, security guarantees, negotiated borders with land swaps, compensation for refugees resettled in Palestine or third countries, and a shared arrangement for Jerusalem. This framework has been accepted in principle by Israeli governments and rejected or ignored by Palestinian leadership at decisive moments.

Israel's peace-oriented governments have consistently included Palestinian statehood in their vision. Rabin was assassinated for pursuing Oslo. Barak offered a state and paid politically. Olmert offered the most generous package in history. The Abraham Accords of 2020 demonstrate that regional peace is achievable when partners are willing.

Hamas's vision — enshrined in its founding documents — is a Muslim state from the river to the sea with no Jewish sovereignty. This is not a negotiating position; it is an eliminationist program. The Palestinian Authority continues to name streets and squares after terrorists who killed Israeli civilians. Israel's vision, constrained by domestic politics, is the only one that contains the structural possibility of coexistence.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Stance
A just peace must be built on international law, equality, and human rights — not on the current power imbalance. It requires full end of the military occupation, recognition of Palestinian self-determination and statehood, a just resolution of the refugee question per Resolution 194, and equal rights for all people living between the river and the sea.

The Arab Peace Initiative of 2002, endorsed by all 22 Arab League states, offered Israel full normalization with the entire Arab and Muslim world in exchange for withdrawal to 1967 borders and a just refugee settlement. Israel never formally responded. The Palestinian vision is rooted in international law and UN resolutions. The status quo that Israel maintains — permanent occupation, expanding settlements — offers neither justice nor lasting peace.

### 🟦 PRO-ISRAEL — Rebuttal
The Arab Peace Initiative was not rejected outright — Israel engaged it and identified fundamental problems, including the right of return clause that would demographically end Israel as a Jewish state. "Full normalization for 1967 borders" also included demands to hand Jerusalem's eastern half during the height of the Second Intifada. The one-state solution with "equal rights" is a formula that in practice ends Jewish self-determination — the one form of national self-determination the opponent apparently considers illegitimate. A negotiated two-state solution remains the only framework that honors both peoples' claims.

### 🟥 PRO-PALESTINE — Rebuttal
The Clinton Parameters were never formally accepted by Israel — Barak's government was collapsing and no written Israeli acceptance was deposited. Even those parameters left Israel controlling Palestinian borders, airspace, and requiring military presence in the Jordan Valley — not full sovereignty. The "river to sea" phrase the opponent uses to condemn Palestinians is simultaneously used by Israeli coalition ministers — Finance Minister Smotrich and National Security Minister Ben-Gvir openly call for annexation and ethnic cleansing. If eliminationist rhetoric disqualifies a side, address Israeli ministers first. 57 years of settlement expansion during every "negotiation" demonstrates which party forecloses the two-state solution on the ground.
