# ROUND 1 — STANCES

---

## PRO-ISRAEL (FOR)

### Q1: Who has the stronger historical and legal claim to the land between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea?

Israel's claim rests on an unbroken 3,000-year connection to the land, beginning with the ancient Israelite kingdoms, documented extensively in archaeology, scripture, and contemporaneous records from Assyrian, Babylonian, and Roman sources. Jews maintained continuous presence even after the Roman expulsions, with communities in Jerusalem, Safed, Tiberias, and Hebron persisting for millennia. The modern legal claim is anchored in the 1917 Balfour Declaration, incorporated into the 1920 San Remo Resolution, which has the force of international treaty law. The League of Nations Mandate for Palestine (1922) explicitly recognized "the historical connexion of the Jewish people with Palestine" and called for reconstituting their national home there. Under the legal doctrine of uti possidetis juris, these mandate boundaries carry forward into successor states. UN General Assembly Resolution 181 (1947) then proposed partition, which the Jewish Agency accepted. The Arab side rejected it and launched a war of annihilation. Israel declared independence on legal territory allotted to it, and its sovereignty was validated by UN admission in 1949. No independent Palestinian state ever existed on this land — it passed from Ottoman to British to Jordanian/Egyptian control. The Jewish people are the indigenous population who returned to their ancestral homeland through lawful, internationally sanctioned means. No other nation on earth has a comparably documented, continuous, legally ratified claim to its territory.

### Q2: Is Israel's military occupation of the West Bank and blockade of Gaza justified on security grounds?

Absolutely. Israel's security measures are direct responses to existential and ongoing threats. The West Bank's strategic topography overlooks Israel's narrow coastal plain, where 70% of the population and critical infrastructure are concentrated — at points, Israel is only 9 miles wide. Before 1967, Arab armies used these highlands to shell Israeli towns. Israel acquired the West Bank in a defensive war it did not initiate. Every time Israel has withdrawn from territory without security guarantees, the result has been escalation: the 2005 complete withdrawal from Gaza — dismantling every settlement and military post — was met not with peace but with Hamas's seizure of power in 2007 and over 20,000 rockets fired at Israeli civilians since. The blockade was imposed only after Hamas, a designated terrorist organization by the US, EU, and others, took control and began importing Iranian weapons. Israel permits humanitarian goods and coordinates aid transfers; the blockade targets weapons and dual-use materials. In the West Bank, the security barrier — built during the Second Intifada when suicide bombings killed over 1,000 Israeli civilians — reduced attacks by over 90%. These are not punitive measures; they are responses calibrated to specific, demonstrated threats. International law under Article 51 of the UN Charter recognizes the inherent right of self-defense. No nation facing thousands of rockets, terror tunnels, and repeated calls for its destruction from neighboring entities would act differently.

### Q3: Does Israel meet the criteria of an apartheid state in its treatment of Palestinians?

No, and this charge is a dangerous distortion that trivializes actual apartheid. In South Africa, apartheid was a system of racial supremacy codified in law against a country's own citizens based on skin color. Israel is a multi-ethnic democracy where Arab citizens — 21% of the population — have full and equal legal rights. Arab Israelis vote, serve in the Knesset, sit on the Supreme Court (Justice Salim Joubran served for years), hold senior military and diplomatic posts, attend universities alongside Jewish students, and use the same hospitals, buses, and beaches. The Ra'am party, an Arab Islamist party, was part of a governing coalition in 2021. Arabic is an officially recognized language. The situation in the West Bank involves a territorial dispute and a military administration over a non-citizen population in disputed territory — a situation that exists because of unresolved conflict and repeated rejection of statehood offers, not because of racial ideology. Conflating a security-driven military administration in contested territory with a system built on racial supremacy is analytically bankrupt. Amnesty and HRW's "apartheid" framing relies on redefining the term beyond its established legal meaning in the 1973 Apartheid Convention, which requires racial domination by one group over another within a single state. Israel and the West Bank are not a single sovereign entity. The apartheid label is political weaponry, not legal analysis.

### Q4: Are Palestinian armed resistance movements justified under international law as liberation struggles?

International law does not provide a blank check for violence under the banner of "liberation." While the right to self-determination is recognized, the means employed must comply with international humanitarian law — the laws of armed conflict. Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad, and similar organizations systematically violate every core principle: they deliberately target civilians (the defining act of terrorism), use human shields by embedding military infrastructure in hospitals, schools, and mosques (documented extensively by the UN itself), fire rockets indiscriminately at population centers, carry out suicide bombings in buses and restaurants, and on October 7, 2023, perpetrated the largest massacre of Jews since the Holocaust — murdering over 1,200 people, including babies, taking 250 hostages, and committing systematic sexual violence. None of this is protected resistance. Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions, Article 44, requires combatants to distinguish themselves from civilians and to direct operations only against military targets. These groups do the opposite on both counts. Furthermore, their stated objective is not liberation but elimination — Hamas's charter calls for Israel's destruction and historically cited antisemitic tropes. A movement whose methods are war crimes and whose goal is genocide cannot claim the moral or legal mantle of legitimate resistance. The Palestinian people deserve leaders who pursue their rights through law, negotiation, and nonviolence — not through atrocities.

### Q5: Has the international community been fair and balanced in its approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict?

The international community's treatment of Israel has been profoundly and demonstrably biased. The UN General Assembly passes more resolutions condemning Israel each year than all other countries combined — more than North Korea, Syria, Iran, and China together. The UN Human Rights Council, which has included members like Venezuela, Libya, and China, maintains a permanent "Agenda Item 7" dedicated solely to Israel — the only country-specific standing item. No other nation on earth receives this treatment. UNESCO has passed resolutions erasing Jewish connections to the Temple Mount and Hebron, referring to Judaism's holiest sites only by Arabic names. UNRWA, the refugee agency uniquely created for Palestinians (all other refugees worldwide are handled by UNHCR), has been repeatedly found to employ Hamas members and allow its facilities to be used for military purposes. The International Court of Justice advisory proceedings have been structurally one-sided, with panels including judges from states that do not recognize Israel's existence. Meanwhile, the EU funds NGOs that promote BDS and delegitimization. The Durban Conference in 2001 devolved into open antisemitism. This systematic singling-out has not advanced peace — it has entrenched Palestinian maximalism by signaling that international institutions will pressure only Israel. True fairness would apply equal scrutiny to Palestinian leadership, hold Hamas accountable for its war crimes, and condition aid on institutional reform and the renunciation of violence.

### Q6: Is the two-state solution still viable, and which side bears more responsibility for its failure?

The two-state solution remains Israel's stated policy, and its repeated failure lies overwhelmingly with Palestinian leadership's rejection of concrete offers. The historical record is unambiguous: In 1937, Jews accepted the Peel Commission partition; Arabs rejected it. In 1947, Jews accepted UN Resolution 181; Arabs rejected it and launched a war. In 2000 at Camp David, Prime Minister Barak offered Arafat approximately 92% of the West Bank, all of Gaza, a capital in East Jerusalem, and a $30 billion refugee compensation fund — Arafat said no and launched the Second Intifada. In 2001 at Taba, Israel offered even more — rejection again. In 2008, Prime Minister Olmert offered 93.7% of the West Bank with land swaps, division of Jerusalem, and an international regime for holy sites — President Abbas walked away without a counter-offer, a fact he himself admitted in a 2015 interview. In 2014, the Kerry framework was rejected by the Palestinian side. Each rejection was followed by violence, not counter-proposals. Meanwhile, Israel demonstrated its willingness to trade land for peace by returning the Sinai Peninsula to Egypt — a territory three times Israel's size — and achieving a lasting peace. The pattern is clear: Israel has consistently accepted the principle of territorial compromise; Palestinian leaders have consistently chosen rejection, because accepting any deal requires acknowledging Israel's permanent legitimacy, which their political movements have been unwilling to do.

### Q7: How should the Palestinian refugee right of return be addressed?

The demand for a literal "right of return" for millions of Palestinian descendants into Israel proper is not a peace proposal — it is a demographic mechanism for Israel's elimination as a Jewish state, and all serious parties understand this. The original refugee population of 1948 numbered approximately 700,000. UNRWA's unique practice of extending refugee status to all descendants in perpetuity — applicable to no other refugee population on earth — has inflated that number to over 5.9 million. Under UNHCR rules applied to every other group, the vast majority would have been resettled and de-registered decades ago. It must also be noted that roughly 850,000 Jews were expelled or forced to flee from Arab countries after 1948, losing property and assets estimated in the tens of billions — they were absorbed into Israel with no international agency, no perpetual refugee status, and no demand for return. A just resolution involves compensation, resettlement in a future Palestinian state, absorption in host countries willing to grant citizenship, and international funding for rehabilitation — not the dissolution of Israel. This is exactly the framework that resolved every other refugee crisis of the 20th century, from the population exchanges between Greece and Turkey to the partition of India. Insisting on "return" to a country that would cease to be itself is not a right — it is an instrument of national destruction dressed in humanitarian language.

### Q8: Is the expansion of Israeli settlements in the West Bank a legitimate expression of sovereignty or a violation of international law?

The legal status of settlements is far more contested than the conventional narrative suggests. The West Bank was never the sovereign territory of any Palestinian state — it was illegally occupied by Jordan from 1948 to 1967 (recognized by only two countries), and before that it was British Mandate territory where Jewish settlement was explicitly encouraged under Article 6 of the Mandate. When Israel took the territory in a defensive war in 1967, there was no recognized sovereign from whom it was "taken." The applicability of the Fourth Geneva Convention's Article 49(6) — which prohibits an occupying power from transferring its population into occupied territory — is legally debatable because that provision was drafted to address forcible transfers like Nazi deportations, not voluntary civilian movement. Distinguished legal scholars like Eugene Rostow, former US Undersecretary of State and a drafter of Resolution 242, argued that Jews have a legal right to settle anywhere in the former Mandate territory west of the Jordan. That said, Israel's own political mainstream recognizes that any peace agreement will involve territorial compromise, as demonstrated by the dismantling of settlements in Sinai and Gaza. The settlement question is ultimately a final-status negotiation issue. But characterizing all Israeli presence beyond the 1949 armistice lines — which were explicitly designated as non-permanent boundaries — as categorically "illegal" is a legal conclusion that prejudges the most contested questions in the dispute.

### Q9: What role does each side's governance (Israeli democracy vs. Hamas/PA leadership) play in perpetuating or resolving the conflict?

Governance is perhaps the most underexamined dimension of this conflict, and the contrast is stark. Israel is the Middle East's only fully functioning liberal democracy — with an independent judiciary that regularly rules against the government and military (including on settlement and barrier issues), a free press, protection of minority and LGBTQ+ rights, regular free elections, and peaceful transfers of power. This institutional framework means Israeli leaders are accountable to their citizens and constrained by law. On the Palestinian side, the PA under Mahmoud Abbas is in the 19th year of a four-year presidential term — no elections since 2006. The PA security services suppress dissent, jail journalists, and torture detainees, as documented by Human Rights Watch. Hamas, which won the 2006 legislative election and then seized Gaza by force in 2007 by throwing Fatah members off rooftops, runs an Islamist authoritarian regime that executes political opponents, enforces religious law, diverts humanitarian aid to military purposes, and has built a vast tunnel network while Gazan civilians lack basic infrastructure. Billions in international aid have been funneled into weapons and tunnels rather than schools and hospitals. This governance failure is not peripheral — it is central. Peace requires a partner capable of making and enforcing agreements. Israel has demonstrated this capacity with Egypt and Jordan. The absence of accountable, democratic Palestinian governance remains one of the most significant structural barriers to resolution.

### Q10: What would a just and lasting peace look like, and which side's vision comes closer to achieving it?

A just and lasting peace must be built on mutual recognition, security, and institutional legitimacy — and Israel's vision aligns far more closely with these requirements. Israel has consistently accepted the principle of two states for two peoples, offered extensive territorial compromises, and proven through treaties with Egypt (1979) and Jordan (1994) and the Abraham Accords (2020) that it is capable of making and sustaining peace when met with genuine partners. Israel's vision includes: a demilitarized Palestinian state in the vast majority of the West Bank and Gaza with negotiated land swaps, a shared or divided Jerusalem with access to holy sites for all faiths, compensation and resettlement for refugees, and robust security arrangements. This framework preserves both peoples' self-determination and dignity. The dominant Palestinian factions' vision, by contrast, ranges from Hamas's explicit call for Israel's destruction and an Islamic state "from the river to the sea" to the PA's insistence on maximal demands — full 1967 borders, all of East Jerusalem, and mass refugee return — that have been rejected at every negotiation because they are designed to be unacceptable. Peace requires compromise from both sides, but it also requires choosing coexistence over elimination. Israel has made that choice repeatedly. The path to peace runs through Palestinian acceptance of Israel's permanent legitimacy, the building of democratic institutions, the disarmament of terrorist factions, and a shared commitment to the principle that two peoples can live side by side in security and mutual respect. That is Israel's offer. It has been on the table for decades.

## PRO-PALESTINE (AGAINST)

### Q1: Who has the stronger historical and legal claim to the land between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea?

Palestinians have the stronger claim, rooted in continuous habitation spanning millennia. The indigenous Arab population of Palestine lived, farmed, and built communities on this land for centuries before the Zionist movement emerged in late 19th-century Europe. The 1947 UN Partition Plan (Resolution 181) was adopted without the consent of the majority population — Palestinians constituted two-thirds of the population yet were allocated only 43% of the land. No legitimate legal framework allows an external body to partition an inhabited territory against the will of its native inhabitants. The principle of self-determination, enshrined in the UN Charter and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, entitles the Palestinian people to sovereignty over their homeland. The Balfour Declaration of 1917, which promised a "national home" for Jewish people in Palestine, was issued by a colonial power (Britain) that had no sovereign right over the territory and explicitly disregarded the political rights of the existing population. Historical presence of Jewish kingdoms thousands of years ago does not override the rights of a living, continuous population — no modern legal system grants territorial claims based on ancient ancestry. The Palestinians are the people of the land, and international law supports their claim.

### Q2: Is Israel's military occupation of the West Bank and blockade of Gaza justified on security grounds?

No. Security concerns, however real, do not grant any state the legal right to maintain a 57-year military occupation in violation of the Fourth Geneva Convention. The occupation is not a temporary security measure — it is a permanent infrastructure of control involving over 700 checkpoints, a separation wall declared illegal by the International Court of Justice in 2004, and a permit regime that governs every aspect of Palestinian life. The blockade of Gaza, in place since 2007, constitutes collective punishment of over two million civilians — a clear violation of Article 33 of the Fourth Geneva Convention. The UN, the International Committee of the Red Cross, and numerous human rights organizations have stated that the blockade causes a humanitarian catastrophe. Security cannot be achieved through domination; decades of occupation have produced only more resistance and instability. Israel's own security establishment figures, including former heads of Shin Bet featured in the documentary "The Gatekeepers," have acknowledged that the occupation is unsustainable and counterproductive to genuine security. True security comes through justice and rights, not through the barrel of a gun pointed at a captive civilian population.

### Q3: Does Israel meet the criteria of an apartheid state in its treatment of Palestinians?

Yes. Multiple authoritative bodies have reached this conclusion after rigorous legal analysis. Amnesty International's 2022 report, Human Rights Watch's 2021 report, and the UN Economic and Social Commission for Western Asia have all independently concluded that Israel's treatment of Palestinians meets the legal definition of apartheid under the 1973 Apartheid Convention and the Rome Statute. The evidence is systemic: two populations live under the same territorial control but are governed by entirely separate legal systems — Israeli settlers in the West Bank enjoy full civil rights and protections under Israeli law while Palestinians beside them live under military law with no political rights. The Nation-State Law of 2018 constitutionally enshrines Jewish supremacy, declaring that the right to self-determination in Israel is "unique to the Jewish people," formally excluding the 20% Palestinian citizen minority. In the West Bank, separate roads, separate courts, separate water allocations, and separate freedom of movement create a reality that South African anti-apartheid leaders, including Archbishop Desmond Tutu, explicitly compared to — and said was worse than — the apartheid they experienced. When the defining characteristics are institutionalized racial domination and systematic oppression, the legal term is apartheid, regardless of how uncomfortable that word may be.

### Q4: Are Palestinian armed resistance movements justified under international law as liberation struggles?

International law is clear: peoples under colonial or foreign occupation have the right to resist, including through armed struggle. UN General Assembly Resolution 3236 (1974) explicitly reaffirms the Palestinian people's right to self-determination and their right to regain their rights "by all means available to them." Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions recognizes wars of national liberation as international armed conflicts, granting combatant status to those fighting against colonial domination, alien occupation, and racist regimes. This legal framework exists precisely because the international community recognized that occupied peoples cannot be denied the right to resist while simultaneously being denied political avenues for freedom. The framing of Palestinian resistance as mere "terrorism" strips context from cause: resistance movements arise because of occupation, not in a vacuum. That said, international humanitarian law demands that all parties — including resistance movements — distinguish between military and civilian targets. The legal right to resist does not extinguish the obligation to follow the laws of war. But the right of resistance itself is firmly grounded in international law, and denying it to Palestinians while their occupation continues is a double standard that privileges the occupier's violence over the occupied's struggle for freedom.

### Q5: Has the international community been fair and balanced in its approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict?

Absolutely not. The international community's approach has been defined by a profound structural imbalance that consistently shields Israel from accountability. The United States has exercised its UN Security Council veto over 50 times to block resolutions critical of Israel — more than on any other single issue. This veto power has rendered the primary body responsible for international peace and security impotent on Palestine. Despite the ICJ's 2004 advisory opinion declaring the separation wall illegal, and despite over 140 UN General Assembly resolutions, Israel faces no enforcement mechanisms, no sanctions, and no material consequences. Compare this to how the international community responded to Iraq's occupation of Kuwait — with immediate military action — or to Russia's actions in Ukraine — with sweeping sanctions. The inconsistency is glaring. The United States provides Israel approximately $3.8 billion annually in military aid, making it complicit in the occupation. European nations, while occasionally expressing concern, continue robust trade and diplomatic relations. The Palestinian people have been forced to negotiate for their basic rights with the very power that occupies them, while that power's chief patron serves as the sole mediator. This is not balance — it is structural injustice codified into the diplomatic process.

### Q6: Is the two-state solution still viable, and which side bears more responsibility for its failure?

Israel bears overwhelming responsibility for the death of the two-state solution. Since the Oslo Accords of 1993, the number of Israeli settlers in the West Bank and East Jerusalem has grown from approximately 250,000 to over 700,000. Every Israeli government — left, right, and center — has expanded settlements, carving the would-be Palestinian state into disconnected enclaves that no honest observer can call viable. The West Bank today resembles an archipelago: Areas A, B, and C fragment Palestinian territory such that Israel controls over 60% of the land directly. Successive Israeli prime ministers — Netanyahu most explicitly — have declared their opposition to Palestinian statehood. The current Israeli government includes ministers who openly advocate annexation and ethnic transfer. Palestinians accepted the two-state framework at enormous cost, conceding 78% of historic Palestine and recognizing Israel within its 1967 borders. The PLO's recognition of Israel in 1993 was a historic concession that was never reciprocated with recognition of a Palestinian state. If the two-state solution is dead, the autopsy shows that Israeli settlement expansion was the cause of death. What remains is a one-state reality in which millions of Palestinians live without rights — and that reality demands a solution grounded in equality for all.

### Q7: How should the Palestinian refugee right of return be addressed?

The right of return is not a negotiating chip — it is an individual human right enshrined in international law. UN General Assembly Resolution 194, passed in 1948 and reaffirmed annually, states that refugees wishing to return to their homes and live at peace should be permitted to do so, and compensation should be paid for the property of those choosing not to return. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Article 13) guarantees every person the right to return to their country. Approximately 750,000 Palestinians were expelled or fled during the 1948 Nakba, and today their descendants number over 5.9 million registered refugees — the longest-standing unresolved refugee crisis in modern history. Their villages were systematically destroyed; over 400 Palestinian communities were depopulated and erased. Meanwhile, Israel's Law of Return grants any Jewish person anywhere in the world — with no prior connection to the land — automatic citizenship, while denying the same right to people born there and their children. This is not a demographic question; it is a moral and legal one. No peace can be considered just if it requires Palestinians to permanently forfeit a right that every other refugee population in history has been granted. The right of return must be acknowledged, and practical mechanisms — return, compensation, or resettlement with full consent — must honor each refugee's individual choice.

### Q8: Is the expansion of Israeli settlements in the West Bank a legitimate expression of sovereignty or a violation of international law?

This is not a matter of debate — it is a matter of settled law. Article 49 of the Fourth Geneva Convention explicitly prohibits an occupying power from transferring its own civilian population into occupied territory. The International Court of Justice affirmed this in its 2004 advisory opinion. UN Security Council Resolution 2334, passed in 2016 with US abstention, declared settlements a "flagrant violation" of international law with "no legal validity." Every single country in the world, including Israel's closest allies, officially considers the settlements illegal — Israel stands alone in its contrary position. Over 700,000 settlers now live in the West Bank and East Jerusalem, on land confiscated from Palestinian owners through military orders, discriminatory zoning, and declaration of "state land." Settlements are not random; they are strategically placed to control water resources, hilltops, and transportation corridors, making a contiguous Palestinian state physically impossible. Settlement infrastructure — including settler-only roads, military installations, and the separation wall — consumes vast tracts of Palestinian land. This is not sovereignty; it is colonization. The settlements are the single greatest obstacle to peace, and their continued expansion demonstrates that Israel's actions directly contradict its stated commitment to a negotiated resolution.

### Q9: What role does each side's governance (Israeli democracy vs. Hamas/PA leadership) play in perpetuating or resolving the conflict?

The framing of Israel as a democracy is itself misleading when millions of people under its effective control cannot vote for the government that controls every aspect of their lives. A democracy that rules over a subject population without enfranchising them is, by definition, not a full democracy — it is an ethnocracy. Israeli governance has consistently empowered the settlement movement, passed discriminatory laws like the Nation-State Law, and maintained the architecture of occupation through every government coalition. Israel directly contributed to Hamas's rise — Israeli officials, including Brigadier General Yitzhak Segev, have acknowledged that Israel supported Hamas in its early years as a counterweight to the secular PLO. Palestinian governance challenges are real but must be understood in context: the PA operates under occupation with no sovereignty, controlling neither borders, airspace, nor resources. When Palestinians held democratic elections in 2006 and Hamas won, the international community rejected the result and imposed a devastating blockade. Palestinians were told to embrace democracy and then punished for its outcome. Governance failures among Palestinians are a symptom of occupation, not its cause. No people can build functional democratic institutions while living under military rule. Address the occupation, and the conditions for democratic governance emerge.

### Q10: What would a just and lasting peace look like, and which side's vision comes closer to achieving it?

A just peace must be built on three pillars: international law, equality, and human rights — not on the current power imbalance. It requires full end of the military occupation, recognition of Palestinian self-determination and statehood, a just resolution of the refugee question per Resolution 194, and equal rights for all people living between the river and the sea regardless of ethnicity or religion. The Palestinian vision, as articulated through decades of diplomatic engagement, has consistently moved toward compromise: the PLO recognized Israel in 1993, accepted a state on just 22% of historic Palestine, and engaged in every diplomatic framework offered. The Arab Peace Initiative of 2002, endorsed by all 22 Arab League states and 57 OIC member countries, offered Israel full normalization with the entire Arab and Muslim world in exchange for withdrawal to 1967 borders and a just refugee settlement. Israel never formally responded. The Palestinian vision is rooted in international law and UN resolutions; it asks not for the destruction of Israel but for freedom, dignity, and equality. By contrast, the status quo that Israel maintains — permanent occupation, expanding settlements, and no viable Palestinian state — offers neither justice nor lasting peace. History teaches that no system of domination endures forever. Peace built on rights and equality will last; peace built on subjugation will not.

## PRO-ISRAEL (FOR) — Rebuttals

### Rebuttal to Q1:
The claim of "continuous habitation spanning millennia" applies equally — if not more forcefully — to Jews, who maintained unbroken presence in the land (Safed, Hebron, Jerusalem, Tiberias) and whose national, religious, and linguistic identity formed there. The 1947 Partition Plan was rejected by Arab states, not imposed — it was a recommendation that Jews accepted and Arabs answered with war. The argument that two-thirds population deserved proportional land ignores that much of the Jewish allocation was the uninhabitable Negev desert. Furthermore, the Mandate for Palestine (1922), an internationally binding instrument through the League of Nations, explicitly recognized the Jewish people's historical connection and right to reconstitute their national home. Dismissing Jewish sovereignty as "thousands of years ago" while claiming Arab presence as "continuous" applies a selective standard. The Arab population itself grew enormously through immigration during the Ottoman and Mandate periods — the 1931 British census recorded residents from dozens of countries. Rights are not zero-sum: Jewish self-determination does not negate Palestinian claims, but Palestinian claims equally cannot erase Jewish indigeneity.

### Rebuttal to Q2:
The occupation exists because of repeated wars launched against Israel — 1948, 1967, 1973 — not because Israel sought territorial expansion. Israel offered to return territories after 1967 and was met with the Khartoum "Three Nos." The security barrier reduced suicide bombings by over 90% — from hundreds of Israeli civilians killed annually to near zero. The ICJ advisory opinion was non-binding, issued without adversarial proceedings, and rejected by multiple democracies. The Gaza blockade began only after Hamas seized power in 2007 and launched thousands of rockets at Israeli civilians; Egypt maintains the same blockade on its border. Quoting former Shin Bet heads selectively ignores that they unanimously affirm the security threats are real — their critique is strategic, not moral, and none advocate unilateral withdrawal without security guarantees. Israel withdrew entirely from Gaza in 2005, dismantling settlements — the result was not peace but 20,000+ rockets.

### Rebuttal to Q3:
The apartheid label is a political weapon, not a legal finding. South African apartheid was racial segregation by law within a sovereign state. Israel's Arab citizens — 21% of the population — vote, serve in parliament, sit on the Supreme Court, and serve as diplomats and military officers. Justice Joubran, an Arab, served on the Israeli Supreme Court. The Ra'am party sits in governing coalitions. The situation in the West Bank involves a belligerent occupation governed by the law of armed conflict — a fundamentally different legal framework from apartheid. The Nation-State Law, while controversial, does not strip any rights from non-Jewish citizens; it is a declarative constitutional provision similar to dozens of nations defining themselves by ethnic or religious character (Pakistan, Greece, Ireland, Denmark). Amnesty and HRW's reports were criticized by leading legal scholars including Eugene Kontorovich and Avi Bell for conflating distinct legal regimes. Desmond Tutu was a moral voice, not a legal authority, and many South Africans — including anti-apartheid activists like Kenneth Gillingham — explicitly rejected the comparison.

### Rebuttal to Q4:
This argument dangerously conflates a contested legal right to resist occupation with an unlimited license to kill civilians. Nothing in international law permits deliberately targeting civilians — that is terrorism, prohibited under every legal framework. UN Resolution 3236 does not authorize attacks on civilians; Additional Protocol I explicitly requires distinction between combatants and civilians. Hamas, Palestinian Islamic Jihad, and others deliberately target buses, cafes, and music festivals. The October 7 massacre — systematic murder of 1,200 civilians, rape, and hostage-taking — cannot be justified under any legal theory. "Armed struggle" in international law means fighting military targets, not butchering families in their homes. The argument that "resistance arises because of occupation" is an explanation, not a justification — every atrocity in history has a claimed cause. International humanitarian law applies regardless of the justness of the cause.

### Rebuttal to Q5:
The US vetoes resolutions because they are systematically one-sided — the UN General Assembly has passed more resolutions against Israel than against all other nations combined, including North Korea, Syria, and Iran. The Human Rights Council included members like Libya and Venezuela while maintaining a permanent agenda item (Item 7) exclusively targeting Israel — no other country has one. The Iraq and Ukraine comparisons fail: those involved unambiguous cross-border invasions of sovereign states. Israel's situation involves disputed territories from a defensive war with no recognized sovereign predecessor. US aid reflects a strategic alliance — the US provides billions to Egypt, Jordan, and the Palestinian Authority as well. Palestinians are not "forced to negotiate with their occupier" — they have repeatedly refused negotiations, including walking away from offers in 2000 (Camp David), 2001 (Taba), and 2008 (Olmert), and refused to even engage with the 2014 Kerry framework.

### Rebuttal to Q6:
Settlement growth is a legitimate criticism, but attributing "overwhelming responsibility" for failed peace to Israel ignores the historical record. At Camp David (2000), Barak offered 92% of the West Bank, Gaza, and a capital in East Jerusalem — Arafat rejected it without a counter-offer. Olmert's 2008 offer included equivalent land swaps for 93.7% of the West Bank — Abbas never responded. The claim "Palestinians accepted the two-state framework at enormous cost" ignores that the PLO's recognition of Israel was conditional and accompanied by continued incitement and payments to families of terrorists (the "pay-for-slay" program, documented by the PA's own budgets). The claim Israel "never reciprocated" recognition is factually wrong — the Oslo Accords explicitly constituted mutual recognition, and Israel recognized the PLO as the representative of the Palestinian people. Hamas, which governs Gaza, explicitly rejects Israel's existence. Responsibility must be shared.

### Rebuttal to Q7:
Resolution 194 is a General Assembly recommendation, not binding law, and its text says refugees "wishing to return and live at peace with their neighbours should be permitted to do so" — the peace condition has never been met by those advocating Israel's destruction. No refugee population in history has maintained refugee status across five generations — UNRWA's unique mandate inflates numbers from 750,000 to 5.9 million. Implementing this "return" would demographically eliminate the Jewish state, which is the transparent goal. The Jewish refugees — 850,000 expelled from Arab countries after 1948, stripped of property and citizenship — are never mentioned. Israel's Law of Return exists because Jewish history proved that without a state guaranteeing refuge, Jews face genocide. Every nation has immigration policy reflecting its character. A two-state solution means Palestinians return to Palestine, Jews to Israel — that is the entire logic of partition.

### Rebuttal to Q8:
Article 49 was drafted to prevent forcible mass transfers like Nazi deportations — not to describe voluntary movement of civilians. Many settlers live in areas of historical Jewish presence (Hebron's Jewish community predates Zionism by centuries, ethnically cleansed in 1929). The legal status is genuinely disputed: Israel relies on the Levy Commission finding that the West Bank is not "occupied territory" in the classical sense because Jordan's sovereignty was never recognized. That said, the Israeli Supreme Court itself has removed illegal outposts and enforced Palestinian property rights. "Every country considers them illegal" is an overstatement — the US under the Trump administration explicitly reversed that position. The settlements occupy approximately 2% of West Bank land area, and every serious peace proposal has included land swaps to accommodate major blocs while providing equivalent territory. Settlements are an obstacle but not the obstacle — there was no peace before 1967 when no settlements existed.

### Rebuttal to Q9:
Israel's 2+ million Arab citizens have full voting rights, political parties, and legal protections — calling Israel "not a democracy" insults them. Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza are governed by the PA and Hamas respectively, not by Israel — they hold (or should hold) elections under their own authorities. The PA has not held elections since 2005 — that is Abbas's choice, not Israel's. The claim Israel "supported Hamas" distorts history: Israel permitted Islamic charitable organizations in the 1980s, before Hamas became a terror group — this was a mistake, not a strategy. When Palestinians held elections in 2006 and chose Hamas — an organization whose charter called for killing Jews — the international community's concern was legitimate. Governance failures — PA corruption, Hamas tyranny, absence of elections, suppression of press and opposition — are choices made by Palestinian leaders. Blaming occupation for every internal failure denies Palestinian agency and infantilizes an entire population.

### Rebuttal to Q10:
A just peace cannot be built on terms that require Israel's demographic destruction (unlimited "return") or reward maximalist demands. The PLO's recognition of Israel was paired with continued glorification of violence in PA textbooks, official media, and the pay-for-slay program — recognition in word without acceptance in deed. The Arab Peace Initiative, while constructive, was presented as a take-it-or-leave-it ultimatum with no room for negotiation, including a refugee clause Israel could never accept. Israel has repeatedly demonstrated willingness to trade land for peace — full withdrawal from Sinai (returning 3x Israel's size), withdrawal from Gaza, and multiple comprehensive offers. The "Palestinian vision rooted in international law" selectively cites only favorable provisions while ignoring Israel's equally valid legal rights under the Mandate, the UN Charter's right to self-defense, and Security Council Resolution 242's deliberate language requiring "secure and recognized boundaries." Peace requires compromise from both sides — not one side's legal maximalism imposed as justice.

## PRO-PALESTINE (AGAINST) — Rebuttals

### Rebuttal to Q1:
A 3,000-year-old religious and historical connection does not translate into a modern legal right to dispossess an existing population. Palestinians are descendants of the diverse peoples who continuously inhabited that land — Canaanites, Philistines, Arabs, and others — for millennia. The Balfour Declaration was issued by a colonial power that had no sovereignty over the land and explicitly stated that "nothing shall be done which may prejudice the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities" — a clause systematically violated. UN Resolution 181 proposed giving 56% of the land to a Jewish population that owned roughly 7% of it and comprised about one-third of the population. That the Arab side rejected this profoundly inequitable proposal is not evidence of irrationality — it is evidence of a people refusing colonial dispossession. The argument that "no independent Palestinian state ever existed" is a colonial framework: most modern states in the Middle East did not exist as sovereign entities before the 20th century, yet no one questions Iraqi or Jordanian peoplehood. Palestinians existed as a distinct community with continuous habitation, and the absence of a Westphalian state does not erase their rights under international law, including the right to self-determination affirmed in the UN Charter itself.

### Rebuttal to Q2:
The framing of Israel's measures as purely "defensive" inverts cause and effect. The occupation of the West Bank began in 1967 and has deepened every year since — the rockets and resistance emerged in response to decades of military rule, land confiscation, and settlement expansion, not the other way around. The 2005 Gaza "withdrawal" removed settlers but maintained total control over airspace, coastline, borders, electromagnetic spectrum, and the population registry — the UN, the ICRC, and virtually every international legal body still considers Gaza occupied. The blockade does not merely target weapons: it has restricted food calories, building materials, medical supplies, and even chocolate and crayons at various points. The World Health Organization, UNICEF, and multiple UN agencies have documented a humanitarian catastrophe. The security barrier's route, which the ICJ ruled illegal in 2004, cuts deep into Palestinian territory, annexing settlements and separating farmers from their fields — 85% of it runs through the West Bank, not along the Green Line. Article 51 does not grant an occupying power unlimited rights against the population it occupies; occupation law under the Fourth Geneva Convention governs that relationship.

### Rebuttal to Q3:
The apartheid designation does not rest solely on the status of Arab citizens within Israel proper — it addresses the system of control Israel exercises over the entire territory between the river and the sea. Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, B'Tselem (an Israeli organization), and the UN's own inquiry have all independently concluded that Israel's policies meet the legal definition of apartheid under the 1973 Convention and the Rome Statute. The test is whether a regime maintains "institutionalized domination by one racial group over another" — and when millions of Palestinians in the West Bank live under military law while their settler neighbors on the same territory enjoy Israeli civil law, that is a textbook two-tier legal system based on ethnicity. Arab Israeli citizens face over 65 discriminatory laws documented by Adalah, the Legal Center for Arab Minority Rights, including the 2018 Nation-State Law that explicitly declared self-determination an exclusive right of the Jewish people. Having token representation in parliament does not negate structural discrimination, just as Black members of South Africa's parliament during the tricameral system did not negate apartheid.

### Rebuttal to Q4:
International humanitarian law is clear: the right to resist occupation exists under the UN Charter, the Geneva Conventions, and multiple General Assembly resolutions including 37/43. Condemning specific tactics does not delegitimize an entire people's right to resistance. The opponent focuses exclusively on Palestinian violations while ignoring that Israel has killed vastly more civilians in every conflict — including over 40,000 Palestinians in Gaza since October 2023 according to the Gaza Health Ministry, figures corroborated by the Lancet and UN agencies. Israel has bombed hospitals, schools, refugee camps, and UN shelters. The ICJ in January 2024 found it plausible that Israel is committing genocide. Invoking October 7th, as horrific as it was, does not provide legal license for collective punishment of an entire population, which is explicitly prohibited under Article 33 of the Fourth Geneva Convention. The framing that Hamas represents all Palestinian resistance is reductive — Palestinian civil society, nonviolent movements, legal advocacy, and diplomatic efforts have been systematically suppressed by Israel for decades.

### Rebuttal to Q5:
The frequency of UN resolutions against Israel reflects the scale and duration of its violations, not institutional bias. Israel has been in continuous violation of international law for over 57 years of occupation — longer than any other military occupation in modern history. The argument amounts to: "So many people condemn us that the condemnation itself must be wrong." South Africa made identical arguments during apartheid. UNRWA was created because Israel refused to allow Palestinian refugees to return, creating an ongoing crisis requiring an ongoing agency. Attacking UNRWA's employees — even if individual cases are substantiated — does not discredit the agency serving two million refugees in Gaza. The ICJ is the principal judicial organ of the UN, and dismissing its rulings because of the nationality of individual judges undermines the entire international legal order. Notably, the ICJ's 2004 advisory opinion on the wall was near-unanimous (14-1), and its 2024 advisory opinion declaring the occupation itself illegal passed with overwhelming majority. The real question is: why does Israel enjoy such impunity despite this consensus?

### Rebuttal to Q6:
This "generous offers rejected" narrative has been dismantled by participants themselves. At Camp David 2000, chief US negotiator Robert Malley and Israeli negotiator Shlomo Ben-Ami both confirmed that no formal written offer was ever made, and that the proposal did not constitute a contiguous, viable state — it would have been divided into cantons by settlement blocs and Israeli-controlled roads. The "92%" figure is misleading: it excluded East Jerusalem, the Jordan Valley, and used inflated baseline calculations. The Olmert offer was never formalized in writing, and Olmert himself was under criminal indictment at the time, lacking political authority to deliver. Meanwhile, Israel has consistently rejected Palestinian offers: the 2002 Arab Peace Initiative — offering full normalization with the entire Arab world in exchange for withdrawal to 1967 lines — was ignored by Israel. The PLO recognized Israel in 1993 on 78% of historic Palestine and got deepening occupation in return. While these "offers" were being discussed, settlement construction never stopped — Israel's settler population grew from 200,000 during Oslo to over 700,000 today. Actions speak louder than negotiation theater.

### Rebuttal to Q7:
The right of return is enshrined in international law. UN Resolution 194 specifically calls for the return of Palestinian refugees, and this is consistent with Article 13 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights: "Everyone has the right to leave any country, including his own, and to return to his country." The claim that this is a "demographic mechanism" reveals the underlying ethno-nationalist logic: that maintaining a Jewish demographic majority justifies denying millions of people their legal rights. The comparison to Jewish refugees from Arab countries is a deflection — those refugees deserve justice too, but their displacement does not cancel Palestinian rights. UNRWA's mandate to include descendants is not unique; the United States granted citizenship to all descendants of its refugee populations, and statelessness is inherited worldwide when no state grants nationality. Palestinians remain refugees precisely because their right to return has been denied and no state has been created to absorb them. The 850,000 Jewish refugees were given Israeli citizenship; Palestinian refugees were given nothing. Suggesting "compensation and resettlement" as a substitute for return is asking Palestinians to accept permanent ethnic cleansing for the comfort of their dispossessors.

### Rebuttal to Q8:
The argument that settlements are legal because "Jordan illegally occupied the West Bank" is self-defeating: if Jordan's occupation was illegal, Israel's is too, and the territory belongs to the Palestinian people pending self-determination. The Fourth Geneva Convention Article 49(6) states: "The Occupying Power shall not deport or transfer parts of its own civilian population into the territory it occupies." The ICJ in 2004, the International Committee of the Red Cross, every single EU member state, the United States (until a brief Trump-era deviation), and even Israel's own legal counsel Theodor Meron in 1967 all concluded that settlements violate international law. Meron's memo was classified precisely because it was inconvenient. Citing Eugene Rostow, a single dissenting legal voice, against this wall of consensus is like citing a climate denier against the IPCC. Calling settlements a "final-status issue" is a strategy for running out the clock: Israel has built 300+ settlements and outposts housing 700,000 settlers while claiming the matter is still "under negotiation." The physical facts being created on the ground are designed to make a Palestinian state impossible.

### Rebuttal to Q9:
Describing Israel as a "liberal democracy" while it maintains the longest military occupation in modern history over five million people who cannot vote for the government that controls their lives is a fundamental contradiction. A democracy that rules over millions of disenfranchised subjects is not a democracy — it is a democracy for some and a military dictatorship for others. Yes, Palestinian governance has serious democratic deficits — but Israel has actively undermined Palestinian democratic development. Israel arrested elected Hamas legislators after the 2006 elections, imposed a siege on Gaza when it didn't like the election results, and has systematically weakened the PA to ensure it cannot function as a credible governing body. Former Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu openly stated his strategy of bolstering Hamas to prevent a unified Palestinian leadership capable of demanding statehood. Blaming Palestinians for their governance failures while maintaining a 57-year occupation that controls their tax revenue, borders, movement, and infrastructure is blaming the prisoner for the condition of the cell.

### Rebuttal to Q10:
Israel has never accepted a viable two-state solution. Every Israeli proposal has demanded: no Palestinian military, Israeli control of borders and airspace, annexation of major settlement blocs, no return to 1967 lines, and Israeli sovereignty over Jerusalem — in other words, a state in name only, a Bantustan. Meanwhile, settlement expansion has accelerated under every Israeli government, left and right. The Abraham Accords normalized relations with countries that were never at war with Israel and explicitly sidelined Palestinian rights — they are not evidence of peace-making, they are evidence of bypassing the core conflict. The phrase "from the river to the sea" is invoked as a Hamas slogan, but senior Israeli ministers — including Finance Minister Smotrich and National Security Minister Ben Gvir, who sit in the current governing coalition — openly advocate for annexation of the entire West Bank and denial of Palestinian statehood. The Likud Party's own charter rejects a Palestinian state west of the Jordan River. Peace requires Israel to end the occupation, dismantle settlements, and recognize Palestinian sovereignty — not demand that the occupied population prove its worthiness to the occupier.
